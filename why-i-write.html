Why I Write

We all believe we have important ideas. But if we attempt to write them down, we struggle to express them clearly. The issue is not a lack of ideas; it is a lack of clarity. Without clarity, we do not really know what we think; we roam blindly through life in a muddle of half-formed ideas. Writing develops clarity and, with that, authenticity.

Before writing an essay, I have to understand which topics matter enough to write about. I use “understand” and not “decide” or “research” because it is not a choice to be made or a factual answer to be found; it is a process of self-evaluation and self-discovery: it forces me to reflect on what is important in my life and identify the problems worth solving. Completing this step, I have a list of topics requiring clarity.

After choosing one, I brainstorm relevant ideas and write them out. This reveals blindspots and weaker ideas to be discarded. It also allows me to strengthen promising ideas and, when I discover synergies, connect them. If I am properly grounded and not writing to self-affirm but to discover, I spend days reflecting on opposing views and counter-arguments. With a matured perspective, I then reflect on my thesis: what is my overarching idea? Decreasingly chaotic iterations of these improvements and the concluding writing process, of conveying this idea well, give the piece its final structure.

The finished essay is a direct imprint of my mind. I have not merely gained clarity on a topic; I have gained clarity on what I believe and why I believe it, giving rise to confidence and authentic action.
